Good Visualization
Title: “Global Temperature Change (1880–2020)”
Source: NASA Climate Change
Link:
https://climate.nasa.gov/vital-signs/global-temperature/ 
Why is this a good visualization?

This data visualization is a strong example of good practice because it follows fundamental principles outlined in key data visualization literature and aligns with course content on effective design and communication.

Clear Encoding: The line chart uses position on a common scale—the most accurate visual encoding method according to Cleveland and McGill (1984)—to represent temperature anomaly over time. This allows for quick and accurate comparisons of values across years.

Color Use: The red-to-blue color gradient is used purposefully to represent temperature increases and decreases. This use of preattentive attributes (Ware, 2012) makes patterns immediately apparent without requiring effortful decoding.

Narrative Structure: As discussed in class, data visualizations should “tell a story.” This visualization does so through a progressive animation that draws attention to a striking upward trend post-1980. The animation aligns with storytelling principles advocated by Kosara and Mackinlay (2013), who emphasize the importance of temporal flow and engagement in narrative visualizations.

Minimal Cognitive Load: The design adheres to Edward Tufte’s concept of the data-ink ratio, minimizing non-data elements and maximizing data density (Tufte, 1983). There is no 3D distortion, excessive gridlines, or decorative clutter.

Accessibility: The visualization uses a high-contrast palette and simple line marks, improving visual accessibility and usability for diverse audiences, aligning with the course's emphasis on equitable communication.

Conclusion:
This visualization demonstrates how good design can enhance clarity, facilitate understanding, and ethically communicate important global information. Its success lies in adhering to both perceptual accuracy and communicative intent.

Bad Visualization
Title: “Where Americans Spend Their Money”
Source: Fox News Pie Chart (2015)
Link:
https://datavizblog.com/2014/09/06/dataviz-squaring-the-pie-chart/
Why is this a bad visualization?

This visualization is often used in data literacy training as an example of what not to do. It violates several principles of effective data visualization discussed in class and supported by foundational literature.

Incorrect Use of Pie Chart: Pie charts are appropriate only when illustrating parts of a whole (Cairo, 2013). This chart fails this basic criterion because the sum of the slices exceeds 100%. This misleads viewers about the relative importance of each category.

Ineffective Visual Encoding: It relies on angle and area, which are less accurate than position or length for visual comparison (Cleveland & McGill, 1984). Viewers struggle to compare slices accurately, especially those with similar sizes.

Label Overlap and Clutter: The overlapping text and lack of white space violate basic readability guidelines. As Tufte (1983) emphasizes, every component of a visualization should serve the viewer’s understanding; here, labels create confusion.

Color Misuse: The colors used are arbitrary and inconsistent. There’s no grouping or hierarchy, and the choices don’t support accessibility (e.g., for color-blind users). This increases cognitive load, undermining the chart’s communicative purpose.

Lack of Transparency: The chart does not cite sources or define what each category includes (e.g., “Other”), which violates principles of ethical data communication discussed in the course.

No Story or Insight: The visualization doesn’t highlight key takeaways, provide context, or guide interpretation. As Few (2009) explains, data visualizations must go beyond displaying numbers—they must inform.

Conclusion:
This visualization fails to meet core design principles, misrepresents data, and adds confusion rather than clarity. It is an example of how poor visual design can distort understanding and erode trust.
